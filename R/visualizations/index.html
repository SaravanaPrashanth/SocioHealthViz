Project Overview

About

The Life Expectancy Trends Visualisation project visually explores how socio-economic factors and mortality patterns have influenced life expectancy trends in England and Wales. It simplifies complex public health data, making it accessible to a broad audience. The project aims to raise awareness about health disparities

Objectives

The project aims to visualise life expectancy trends, analyze variations by age, sex, and socio-economic status, and highlight disparities in avoidable mortality. Key questions include trends over time, differences by demographic groups, and the impact of socio-economic deprivation.

Methods

Data from health and social sources is used to analyze trends in life expectancy, mortality rates, and socio-economic factors. Visualisations like graphs and charts present the data in an easy-to-understand format, making complex insights more accessible.

Composite Analysis

Comprehensive Health Inequality Analysis

This visualisation combines multiple health metrics to provide a complete picture of health inequalities in England and Wales. It shows the relationships between life expectancy, deprivation levels, and mortality rates across different regions and demographic groups.

Key Insights:

  • Clear correlation between deprivation levels and health outcomes
  • Regional variations in life expectancy between England and Wales
  • Gender-based differences in health outcomes across all metrics
  • Temporal trends showing changes in health inequalities over time

Research Questions & Findings

1. Life Expectancy Trends (2005-2023)

Question: What are the trends in life expectancy for males and females in England and Wales from 2005 to 2023?

Findings: The analysis shows consistently higher life expectancy for females compared to males in both regions. England generally maintains slightly higher life expectancy than Wales. There's a notable upward trend until 2015, followed by a slight decline in recent years, particularly noticeable during 2020. Female life expectancy in England reaches approximately 37 years, while males in Wales show the lowest values at around 33 years.

2. Mortality Rates by Demographics

Question: How do mortality rates vary by age group and sex across different years?

Findings: The box plot reveals that mortality rates generally increase with age across both England and Wales. Males consistently show higher mortality rates than females across all age groups. The disparity becomes more pronounced in older age groups (40-60 years). Both regions show similar patterns, though Wales exhibits slightly higher variability in mortality rates.

3. Avoidable Mortality and Deprivation

Question: What are the disparities in avoidable mortality rates between deprivation deciles in England and Wales from 2011 to 2020?

Findings: There's a clear correlation between deprivation and avoidable mortality rates. The most deprived areas (decile 1) show mortality rates nearly three times higher than the least deprived areas (decile 10). Wales generally shows higher rates across all deciles compared to England, with the gap widening in more deprived areas.

4. Inequality Trends Over Time

Question: How does the slope index of inequality (SII) for avoidable mortality change across deprivation quintiles over time?

Findings: The heatmap shows the evolution of health inequalities from 2005 to 2020. Darker colours indicate higher inequality levels. Both England and Wales show persistent patterns of inequality, with some variations over time. Males generally experience higher inequality levels than females, as indicated by the more intense colours in the male sections of both regions.

Analysis Results

Key Findings

Our analysis reveals several important patterns in health outcomes across England and Wales:

  • Women consistently live longer than men in both England and Wales, with the gap remaining steady over time.
  • There's a concerning link between poverty and health - people in the most deprived areas are three times more likely to die from preventable causes.
  • Health inequalities are more pronounced for men than women, particularly in areas of higher deprivation.
  • The COVID-19 period (2020) showed a noticeable dip in life expectancy across all groups.
  • Wales generally faces greater health challenges than England, with slightly lower life expectancy and higher mortality rates.

These findings suggest we need targeted public health programs, especially in more deprived areas and for male populations, to reduce these health gaps.

Impact

These findings have important implications for public health policy:

  • More resources and healthcare services should be directed to areas with high deprivation levels.
  • Special attention is needed for men's health programs to address their higher mortality rates.
  • Regional differences between England and Wales suggest the need for locally tailored health interventions.
  • Prevention programs should focus on reducing avoidable deaths, particularly in disadvantaged communities.
